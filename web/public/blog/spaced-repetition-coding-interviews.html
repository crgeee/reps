Spaced Repetition for Coding Interviews: A Complete Guide

Published March 2, 2026 · 12 min read

You solved 200 LeetCode problems and forgot most of them

Here is a scenario that should feel familiar. You spend three months grinding LeetCode. You work through blind 75 lists, NeetCode roadmaps, and curated company-tagged problems. You solve 200+ problems. You feel prepared.

Then you sit down for your onsite at a top company, and the interviewer gives you a problem involving a monotonic stack. You solved three of those two months ago. You remember the concept vaguely. You cannot recall the implementation details. You spend 25 minutes floundering through a brute-force approach, and the interviewer moves on.

This is not a skill problem. It is a memory problem. And there is a well-studied, algorithmic solution for it.

The core issue is that most engineers treat interview prep like a checklist. Solve problem, check it off, move on. But solving a problem once does not mean you can solve it again two weeks later. Research on human memory has shown this consistently for over a century: without deliberate review, you forget roughly 70% of new material within 48 hours.

Spaced repetition for coding interviews fixes this by scheduling your reviews at the exact intervals where your memory is about to fade. Instead of re-solving 200 problems randomly, you review the 8 problems that are due today. The result is less total study time, higher retention, and the ability to recall patterns under pressure.

What is spaced repetition?

In 1885, German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus ran memory experiments on himself and discovered what is now called the forgetting curve. After learning something new, your memory of it decays exponentially. Within an hour you have lost about 50%. Within a day, 70%. Within a week, you are down to roughly 20% retention.

But Ebbinghaus also discovered something useful: each time you review material at the right moment (just before you would forget it), the forgetting curve flattens. The memory becomes more durable. After a few well-timed reviews, something you originally forgot in two days now sticks for two weeks. Then two months. Then it is effectively permanent.

Spaced repetition is the practical application of this principle. Instead of massed practice (cramming), you spread your reviews out over increasing intervals. The first review might happen after 1 day. If you remember it well, the next review is in 6 days. Then 15 days. Then 35 days. The intervals grow as your memory strengthens.

The key insight for coding interview prep is this: you do not need to solve every problem again. You only need to solve the problems you are about to forget. A spaced repetition system tells you exactly which ones those are, every single day.

How the SM-2 algorithm works

SM-2 is the algorithm behind Anki and most modern spaced repetition systems. It was created by Piotr Wozniak in 1987, and despite being nearly four decades old, it remains remarkably effective. Here is how it works in plain terms.

Every item you are learning has three key values:

  • Repetitions — how many times in a row you have successfully recalled it
  • Interval — the number of days until your next review
  • Ease factor — a multiplier (starting at 2.5) that controls how fast intervals grow. Higher ease means you find it easier, so reviews spread further apart

After each review, you rate yourself on a 0-5 quality scale:

Rating Meaning
5Perfect response, no hesitation
4Correct after brief hesitation
3Correct but required significant effort to recall
2Incorrect, but the correct answer felt familiar
1Incorrect, remembered after seeing the answer
0Complete blackout, no memory at all

The algorithm then updates the interval based on your rating:

  • If you rated 3 or above (you got it right): your repetition count increases, and the interval grows by multiplying the current interval by your ease factor.
  • If you rated below 3 (you got it wrong): repetitions reset to 0 and the interval resets to 1 day. You start over for that problem.

The ease factor also adjusts after every review, making future intervals longer for easy items and shorter for hard ones.

Here is a simplified TypeScript implementation of the core SM-2 calculation. This is essentially what powers reps, the interview prep tracker this blog is part of:

type Quality = 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5;

function calculateSM2(
  repetitions: number,
  interval: number,
  easeFactor: number,
  quality: Quality
) {
  if (quality >= 3) {
    // Correct response: increase interval
    if (repetitions === 0) {
      interval = 1;        // First success: review tomorrow
    } else if (repetitions === 1) {
      interval = 6;        // Second success: review in 6 days
    } else {
      interval = Math.round(interval * easeFactor);
    }
    repetitions += 1;
  } else {
    // Incorrect: reset and review tomorrow
    repetitions = 0;
    interval = 1;
  }

  // Adjust ease factor based on performance
  easeFactor = Math.max(
    1.3,
    easeFactor + (0.1 - (5 - quality) * (0.08 + (5 - quality) * 0.02))
  );

  const nextReview = new Date();
  nextReview.setDate(nextReview.getDate() + interval);

  return { repetitions, interval, easeFactor, nextReview };
}

Let us trace through a concrete example. Say you solve "Merge Intervals" for the first time and rate yourself a 4 (correct with minor hesitation):

  • Repetitions: 0 → 1. Interval: 1 day. Ease factor adjusts to 2.5.
  • You review it tomorrow and rate a 4 again. Repetitions: 2. Interval: 6 days.
  • Six days later you rate a 5. Repetitions: 3. Interval: 6 × 2.6 = 16 days.
  • Sixteen days later you rate a 3 (struggled but got it). Interval: 16 × 2.36 = 38 days.

After four reviews spread over about three weeks of real time, you will not see this problem again for over a month. Compare that to mindlessly re-solving it every few days. Spaced repetition is efficient because it respects your memory rather than fighting it.

How to apply spaced repetition to coding interview prep

The theory is straightforward. The implementation details matter. Here is how to structure your prep using spaced repetition effectively.

Organize problems by pattern, not by difficulty

The biggest mistake engineers make is sorting problems by difficulty (easy, medium, hard) and grinding through them sequentially. This teaches you to solve specific problems, not to recognize patterns.

Instead, organize your prep around algorithmic patterns:

  • Two pointers — container with most water, 3Sum, trapping rain water
  • Sliding window — longest substring without repeating characters, minimum window substring
  • BFS/DFS — number of islands, word ladder, course schedule
  • Dynamic programming — coin change, longest increasing subsequence, edit distance
  • Binary search — search in rotated sorted array, find minimum in rotated sorted array
  • Monotonic stack — daily temperatures, largest rectangle in histogram
  • Graph algorithms — Dijkstra, topological sort, union-find
  • Tree traversals — serialize/deserialize, lowest common ancestor, path sum
  • Heap/Priority queue — merge k sorted lists, find median from data stream
  • Backtracking — permutations, subsets, N-Queens

When you solve a problem tagged as "sliding window," you are not just learning one solution. You are reinforcing a template that applies to dozens of problems. When spaced repetition schedules a sliding window problem for review, you are reviewing the pattern, not just the specific problem.

Rate your understanding honestly after each problem

The quality rating is the most important input to the SM-2 algorithm. It determines how soon you will see a problem again. Be ruthless with yourself:

  • Rate 5 only if you could solve it in an interview right now, under time pressure, while explaining your approach to someone.
  • Rate 4 if you solved it correctly but had to pause and think about edge cases or time complexity.
  • Rate 3 if you solved it but felt shaky. You would not be confident in an interview setting.
  • Rate 2 or below if you needed hints, looked at the solution, or could not finish within 30 minutes.

The temptation is to rate yourself a 4 when it was really a 3, because seeing the problem again in 1 day feels annoying. Resist this. The algorithm is only as good as your honesty. A dishonest 4 means the problem disappears for two weeks, and you will have forgotten it completely by then.

Trust the schedule

Once you have a spaced repetition system running, your daily workflow becomes simple: open the app, see what is due, work through those problems. That is it.

Do not review problems that are not due. If you solved "Two Sum" last week and rated it a 5, and it is not scheduled for review for another 20 days, leave it alone. Your brain is consolidating that memory. Reviewing it early is wasted time that could be spent on problems you are actually about to forget.

Do add new problems. A healthy prep routine mixes review of old problems (typically 20-40 minutes) with learning new ones (30-60 minutes). The spaced repetition system handles the old problems automatically. You just need to keep feeding it new ones.

Common mistakes with spaced repetition for coding interviews

I have seen these mistakes repeatedly, both in my own prep and from engineers I have coached.

1. Reviewing too often

Some engineers set up a spaced repetition system and then continue to review problems daily regardless of what the schedule says. This defeats the entire purpose. The spaces between reviews are not wasted time. They are when the actual memory consolidation happens. If you review a problem every day, you never give your brain the chance to almost-forget it, which is the event that makes the memory stronger upon recall.

2. Not rating honestly

We covered this above, but it bears repeating because it is the single most common failure mode. If you rate every problem a 4 or 5, the algorithm thinks you are retaining everything and schedules reviews weeks apart. Then when you actually sit down for an interview, you cannot recall the approach for a problem you "solved" three times. The system works only when the inputs are accurate.

3. Skipping the hard ones

When you open your review queue and see an easy linked list problem and a hard dynamic programming problem, the temptation is to do the easy one first, spend too long on it, and "run out of time" for the hard one. This is exactly backwards. The hard problem is the one the algorithm is telling you to review because you are about to forget it. The easy one will take five minutes. Do the hard one first.

4. Adding too many problems too fast

If you add 20 new problems on day one, you will have 20 reviews due on day two, plus whatever new problems you want to learn. By day five, you could have 40+ reviews stacking up and the whole system collapses under its own weight. Start with 3-5 new problems per day maximum. Let the review queue stabilize before adding more.

5. Only reviewing code, not the explanation

Coding interviews are not just about writing correct code. You need to explain your approach, discuss time and space complexity, and walk through examples. When you review a problem, do not just re-code the solution silently. Practice saying out loud: "I would use a sliding window here because..." and "The time complexity is O(n) because each element is visited at most twice." This verbal component is critical and is also subject to the forgetting curve.

Tools for spaced repetition in coding interview prep

You have several options, ranging from fully manual to purpose-built.

Anki

Anki is the most well-known spaced repetition software. It is free, open source, and extremely flexible. You can create flashcards with code snippets, tag them by pattern, and Anki handles the scheduling. The downside is that Anki is designed for flashcard-style recall (read front, think of back), which is not quite the right model for coding problems. You want to actually solve the problem, not just recall the answer. Anki also requires significant setup time to create good cards.

Custom spreadsheets

Some engineers track their problems in a Google Sheet with columns for problem name, pattern, last reviewed date, next review date, and ease factor. This works and gives you full control, but you have to manually calculate the SM-2 intervals (or use formulas). The main risk is that you stop updating the spreadsheet after a few weeks because it is friction-heavy.

An 8-week study plan using spaced repetition

Here is a concrete plan that balances learning new patterns with reviewing old ones. Adjust the time commitment based on your schedule, but do not skip the daily reviews.

Weeks 1-2: Foundation patterns

New problems per day: 2-3
Daily review time: 15-20 minutes
Focus areas: Arrays, strings, hash maps, two pointers, sliding window

Start with the most common patterns. These show up in nearly every interview loop. Solve each problem, rate yourself honestly, and let the SM-2 scheduler do its job. By the end of week two, you should have 25-35 problems in your system and a manageable daily review queue of 5-8 problems.

Weeks 3-4: Trees, graphs, and recursion

New problems per day: 2-3
Daily review time: 20-30 minutes
Focus areas: Binary trees, BFS/DFS, graph traversal, backtracking

Your review queue now includes foundation problems from weeks 1-2 that are coming due. This is where spaced repetition proves its value. You will notice some problems feel easy on review (rate them 4-5 and move on quickly) while others have faded (rate them honestly and let the algorithm reschedule). Spend the remaining time learning tree and graph patterns.

Weeks 5-6: Advanced patterns

New problems per day: 1-2
Daily review time: 30-40 minutes
Focus areas: Dynamic programming, monotonic stack, tries, advanced graph (shortest path, topological sort)

Slow down on new problems. Your review queue is growing and these are the problems that matter most. Dynamic programming in particular requires repeated exposure before the patterns click. Do not be discouraged if you rate DP problems as 2 or 3 several times. That is the system working correctly. It will keep scheduling them until you actually retain them.

Weeks 7-8: System design and mock interviews

New problems per day: 0-1
Daily review time: 30-40 minutes
Focus areas: System design practice, behavioral prep, mock interview simulation

Stop adding many new coding problems. Your review queue should now include 60-80 problems across all patterns, and the daily reviews will keep them fresh. Shift your new-problem time to system design (design a URL shortener, design a chat system, design a rate limiter) and behavioral questions. These also benefit from spaced repetition: add them to your system with the same quality ratings.

By the end of 8 weeks, here is what your system should look like:

  • 60-80 problems tracked with SM-2 data
  • Daily review queue of 5-10 problems (taking 20-30 minutes)
  • High-ease-factor problems (your strong patterns) appearing only every few weeks
  • Low-ease-factor problems (your weak areas) appearing every few days, which is exactly where you need the practice
  • 5-8 system design scenarios in rotation
  • 10-15 behavioral stories refined through repetition

This is dramatically more effective than solving 300 LeetCode problems in random order and hoping for the best. You are not just solving problems. You are building durable, recallable knowledge of patterns that you can deploy under interview pressure.

The bottom line

Spaced repetition is not a hack or a shortcut. It is the application of memory science to a domain where memory matters. Coding interviews test your ability to recall and apply algorithmic patterns under time pressure. That is fundamentally a memory task, and spaced repetition is the most efficient tool we have for memory tasks.

The engineers who succeed in interview loops are not necessarily smarter or more experienced. They are the ones who can recall the right pattern at the right moment. Spaced repetition ensures that you can.

Start today. Pick 3 problems. Solve them. Rate yourself. Let the algorithm schedule your reviews. In two weeks, you will notice the difference. In eight weeks, you will walk into interviews knowing that every pattern you have studied is fresh in your memory, ready to deploy.
