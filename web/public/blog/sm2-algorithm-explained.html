SM-2 Algorithm Explained: How Spaced Repetition Actually Works

Published March 2, 2026 · 12 min read

You have probably heard that spaced repetition is the most efficient way to memorize information long-term. Tools like Anki, SuperMemo, and reps all use it. But when someone says "spaced repetition," what they usually mean is a specific algorithm running under the hood, deciding when you should see each card again.

That algorithm, more often than not, is SM-2.

SM-2 is remarkably simple. The entire thing fits in about 30 lines of code. Yet it has powered billions of review sessions across millions of users for nearly four decades. In this article, we are going to break it down completely: the history, the math, the formulas, and a full working TypeScript implementation you can drop into your own projects.

A Brief History of SM-2

In 1987, a Polish researcher named Piotr Wozniak was trying to solve a practical problem: he was learning English vocabulary and wanted to figure out the optimal time to review each word. Too soon and you waste time. Too late and you have already forgotten it.

Wozniak wrote a series of algorithms, each one refining the last. SM-0 and SM-1 were manual, table-based approaches. SM-2 was the breakthrough. It introduced the concept of an ease factor — a per-item difficulty score that adjusts based on how well you recall the item. Easy items get reviewed less frequently. Hard items come back sooner.

SM-2 became the engine behind SuperMemo 2, one of the first commercial spaced repetition programs. Wozniak went on to develop more sophisticated versions — SM-3 through SM-18 — but SM-2 was the one that escaped into the wild. When Damien Elmes created Anki in 2006, he chose SM-2 as its scheduling algorithm. Since Anki is open source, SM-2 became the de facto standard for spaced repetition worldwide.

Today, despite being nearly 40 years old, SM-2 remains the most widely deployed spaced repetition algorithm in existence. Anki alone has tens of millions of users. Countless other apps — including reps — use it or a close variant.

The Algorithm, Step by Step

SM-2 operates on a simple loop. Each time you review an item, you assign it a quality rating from 0 to 5. The algorithm uses that rating to update three variables, which together determine when you will see the item again.

Quality Ratings (0–5)

The quality rating is your self-assessment of how well you recalled the item. Here is what each score means:

Rating Label Description
0 Complete blackout No memory of the item at all. Total failure.
1 Incorrect, remembered on reveal You got it wrong but recognized the answer immediately when shown.
2 Incorrect, easy on reveal Wrong answer, but the correct one seemed easy once revealed.
3 Correct with serious difficulty You recalled it, but it required significant effort.
4 Correct with hesitation Correct recall after some thinking. The most common "good" rating.
5 Perfect response Instant, effortless recall. You know this cold.

The critical threshold is 3. A quality rating of 3 or above counts as a successful recall — the item's review streak continues. A rating below 3 is a failure — the streak resets and you start over from the beginning.

The Three Key Variables

SM-2 tracks three variables per item:

  • repetitions — How many consecutive successful reviews the item has had. Resets to 0 on any failure (quality < 3).
  • interval — The number of days until the next review. Starts at 1, jumps to 6 after the first success, then grows exponentially via the ease factor.
  • easeFactor — A floating-point multiplier (minimum 1.3) that controls how quickly intervals grow. Starts at 2.5 for all items. Items you consistently rate highly get a higher ease factor and grow to longer intervals faster. Items you struggle with get a lower ease factor, keeping intervals shorter.

The Formulas

After each review, SM-2 updates the variables in two phases.

Phase 1: Update interval and repetitions

If the quality rating is 3 or higher (successful recall): 

if repetitions == 0:  interval = 1
if repetitions == 1:  interval = 6
if repetitions >= 2:  interval = interval * easeFactor

repetitions = repetitions + 1

If the quality rating is below 3 (failed recall): 

repetitions = 0
interval = 1

Phase 2: Update ease factor

Regardless of whether you passed or failed, the ease factor is adjusted using this formula: 

EF' = EF + (0.1 - (5 - q) * (0.08 + (5 - q) * 0.02))

Where EF is the current ease factor and q is the quality rating (0–5). The result is clamped to a minimum of 1.3 to prevent intervals from shrinking too aggressively.

Let's look at what this formula produces for each quality rating:

Quality (q) EF Change Effect
0 -0.80 Massive decrease — item is very hard
1 -0.54 Large decrease
2 -0.32 Moderate decrease
3 -0.14 Small decrease — barely passed
4 +0.00 No change — item is at equilibrium
5 +0.10 Increase — item is becoming easier

Notice that the algorithm is pessimistic by design. Only a perfect score of 5 increases the ease factor. A rating of 4 — which is "correct with some hesitation" — leaves the ease factor unchanged. Anything below 4 decreases it. This asymmetry means that items naturally trend toward harder scheduling over time unless you consistently nail them.

Worked Example: "Binary Search" Card

Let's walk through a concrete example. You have a task card called "Binary Search" and you are going to review it three times over the next few weeks. We will track every variable at each step.

Initial state (before any review): 

repetitions = 0
interval    = 1
easeFactor  = 2.50

Review 1 — Quality: 4 (correct with hesitation)

You see the card "Binary Search." You remember the algorithm: pick the midpoint, compare, recurse on the correct half. You get the time complexity right (O(log n)) but hesitate on the edge case for an empty array. You rate yourself a 4. 

Quality >= 3, so this is a success.
repetitions was 0, so interval = 1 day.
repetitions = 0 + 1 = 1

EF' = 2.50 + (0.1 - (5-4) * (0.08 + (5-4) * 0.02))
    = 2.50 + (0.1 - 1 * (0.08 + 1 * 0.02))
    = 2.50 + (0.1 - 0.10)
    = 2.50 + 0.00
    = 2.50

Result: review again in 1 day. Ease factor unchanged at 2.50.

Review 2 — Quality: 3 (correct with serious difficulty)

One day later. You remember binary search but struggle with the iterative implementation. You eventually get it right but it was a grind. Rating: 3. 

Quality >= 3, so this is a success.
repetitions was 1, so interval = 6 days.
repetitions = 1 + 1 = 2

EF' = 2.50 + (0.1 - (5-3) * (0.08 + (5-3) * 0.02))
    = 2.50 + (0.1 - 2 * (0.08 + 2 * 0.02))
    = 2.50 + (0.1 - 2 * 0.12)
    = 2.50 + (0.1 - 0.24)
    = 2.50 + (-0.14)
    = 2.36

Result: review again in 6 days. Ease factor dropped to 2.36 because the recall was difficult.

Review 3 — Quality: 5 (perfect response)

Six days later. This time binary search clicks instantly. You can write both recursive and iterative versions, handle edge cases, and explain the time complexity without thinking. Rating: 5. 

Quality >= 3, so this is a success.
repetitions was 2, so interval = round(6 * 2.36) = round(14.16) = 14 days.
repetitions = 2 + 1 = 3

EF' = 2.36 + (0.1 - (5-5) * (0.08 + (5-5) * 0.02))
    = 2.36 + (0.1 - 0 * (0.08 + 0))
    = 2.36 + 0.10
    = 2.46

Result: review again in 14 days. Ease factor recovered to 2.46.

After three reviews, your binary search card is scheduled 14 days out and has an ease factor of 2.46. If you keep rating it 5, the next intervals would be roughly 34 days, then 84 days, then 207 days. The exponential growth is the whole point: items you know well quickly move to long intervals, freeing up your daily review time for items you actually need to practice.

Full TypeScript Implementation

Here is a clean, complete implementation of SM-2 in TypeScript. This is the exact algorithm that reps uses to schedule interview prep reviews. 

/** Quality rating from 0 (blackout) to 5 (perfect) */
type Quality = 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5;

interface SM2Result {
  repetitions: number;
  interval: number;
  easeFactor: number;
}

function sm2(
  quality: Quality,
  repetitions: number,
  interval: number,
  easeFactor: number
): SM2Result {
  // Phase 1: Update interval and repetitions
  if (quality >= 3) {
    // Successful recall
    if (repetitions === 0) {
      interval = 1;
    } else if (repetitions === 1) {
      interval = 6;
    } else {
      interval = Math.round(interval * easeFactor);
    }
    repetitions += 1;
  } else {
    // Failed recall — reset
    repetitions = 0;
    interval = 1;
  }

  // Phase 2: Update ease factor (always runs)
  easeFactor = easeFactor + (0.1 - (5 - quality) * (0.08 + (5 - quality) * 0.02));
  easeFactor = Math.max(1.3, easeFactor);
  easeFactor = Math.round(easeFactor * 100) / 100;

  return { repetitions, interval, easeFactor };
}

That is the entire algorithm. Thirty lines of logic. The only subtle detail is rounding the ease factor to two decimal places, which prevents floating-point drift from accumulating over hundreds of reviews. The Math.max(1.3, ...) clamp ensures the ease factor never drops below 1.3, which would cause intervals to shrink on successful reviews.

To use this in practice, you would store the three return values alongside each item and compute the next review date by adding interval days to the current date. When querying for items to review, select everything where nextReview <= today.

Strengths and Weaknesses of SM-2

Strengths

  • Simplicity. The algorithm is trivial to implement, debug, and reason about. There is no machine learning model, no training data, no hidden state. You can trace through the math by hand.
  • Proven track record. SM-2 has been in production use since 1987. Medical students have passed board exams with it. Language learners have acquired fluency with it. The algorithm works.
  • Per-item adaptation. The ease factor adjusts independently for each item, so the algorithm naturally spends more time on things you find difficult and less time on things you find easy.
  • Predictable behavior. Because the algorithm is deterministic and stateless (no cross-item effects), it is easy to test and the user can develop an intuition for how their ratings affect scheduling.
  • Low overhead. Each item requires only three numbers to be stored. There is no need for a review history, no need for aggregate statistics. This makes SM-2 extremely efficient for both storage and computation.

Weaknesses

  • The ease factor death spiral. This is the most well-known problem with SM-2. If you rate an item 3 several times in a row, the ease factor drops steadily (2.50 → 2.36 → 2.22 → 2.08 → 1.94). Lower ease factors mean shorter intervals, which means you see the item more often, which means you are more likely to give it a marginal rating, which drops the ease factor further. Once an item's ease factor hits the 1.3 floor, it is stuck there permanently unless you start rating it 5 consistently.
  • No leech detection. Some items are fundamentally hard and you keep failing them over and over. SM-2 just resets to interval=1 each time. It has no mechanism to flag these "leech" cards so you can rewrite or remove them.
  • No consideration of item difficulty. Every new item starts with the same ease factor of 2.5. SM-2 has no way to predict that "implement a red-black tree" will be harder than "implement a stack" before you have reviewed them.
  • Binary pass/fail threshold. The jump between quality 2 (fail) and quality 3 (pass) is enormous. Fail resets everything to day 1. Pass keeps the streak going. There is no middle ground.
  • No backlog handling. If you skip reviews for a month and come back with 200 overdue items, SM-2 has no opinion about how to prioritize them. It just says they are all due.

Modern Alternatives

Wozniak himself moved far beyond SM-2. His latest algorithms (SM-17 and SM-18) use a complex model of memory based on two variables of memory stability and retrievability, with optimization across the user's entire review history. These algorithms power the commercial SuperMemo application but are not open source and are significantly more complex to implement.

The most significant modern challenger is FSRS (Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler), developed by Jarrett Ye. FSRS uses a machine learning model with four parameters representing memory stability, difficulty, and retrievability. It was adopted by Anki as an alternative scheduler in 2023. Benchmarks show that FSRS achieves better review efficiency than SM-2 — users retain the same amount of information with fewer reviews. FSRS also handles the ease factor death spiral by design, since it does not use a single multiplicative ease factor at all.

Other alternatives include Leitner boxes (simpler than SM-2 but less efficient), Ebisu (a Bayesian approach), and various proprietary algorithms used by apps like Memrise and Duolingo. But none of these has achieved the combination of simplicity, openness, and proven effectiveness that keeps SM-2 in widespread use.

Why SM-2 Is Still the Right Choice for Interview Prep

If FSRS is measurably better, why does reps use SM-2? Context matters.

FSRS shines when you have a large collection — thousands of flashcards — and you need to optimize for minimal daily review time while maintaining high retention across all of them. This is the language-learning scenario: 5,000 vocabulary words, 20 reviews a day, over multiple years.

Interview prep is different. You are not memorizing 5,000 flashcards. You are deeply practicing 200 to 400 problems over a period of weeks or months. Each "review" is not a five-second recall check — it is a 20-minute problem-solving session or a mock behavioral answer. At this scale, the efficiency gains of FSRS over SM-2 are negligible. The difference between "review this problem in 12 days" versus "review this problem in 14 days" does not meaningfully affect your preparation.

What matters for interview prep is:

  • Predictability. You want to understand why a problem is being surfaced today. SM-2 makes this transparent: you rated it a 3 last time, so the ease factor dropped and the interval shortened.
  • Simplicity. You want to trust the system, not debug it. SM-2 is simple enough that you can verify it is doing the right thing.
  • The fail/reset mechanic actually helps. If you cannot solve a problem, you should see it again tomorrow. SM-2's aggressive reset on failure is a feature in this context, not a bug.

The ease factor death spiral is the one legitimate concern, but it is manageable with a small deck. If you notice a problem's ease factor has bottomed out at 1.3, that is useful signal: either you need to break it into subproblems, add better notes, or change your approach to studying it entirely.

SM-2 has been scheduling review sessions for nearly four decades. It is not the most sophisticated algorithm anymore, but it is the most battle-tested. For interview prep specifically — where your deck is small, your stakes are high, and your time horizon is weeks not years — it is still the right tool.
